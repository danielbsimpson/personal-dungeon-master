## Act 0 — The Bargain

### Kumasi's Edge

| Character | Role | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| Elder Kofi | Village elder / context and guide | An elderly man who has lived in Kumasi's Edge his entire life and has long since made peace with what the Silk-Cotton Tree is. He does not warn the player away — he has learned that those who arrive asking for Anansi cannot be dissuaded, only prepared. He explains the three-part ritual at the Crossroads Shrine: an offering of grain, an offering of cloth, and a spoken name — the player's own, said plainly and without theater. He explains that inside the spirit market, Anansi controls everything except the player's own choices, and that this is the only real advantage the player has. He mentions Yaa casually — a weaver who went in three days ago without completing the ritual — and asks, equally casually, that if the player happens to see her, they bring her back. He does not say that she is his granddaughter. She is. A DC 12 Insight check reveals that his composure around her disappearance is a performance — he is afraid, and he has been afraid for three days. He will be beneath the tree when the player returns. He asks nothing else. |

## Act 1 — The Three Tasks

### The Spirit Market (All Locations)

| Character | Role | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| Anansi | The trickster god | Anansi appears first as a small, well-dressed older man in immaculate robes — beautifully maintained cloth in deep gold and brown, with round-lensed spectacles that do not quite sit straight on his face. He has too many eyes behind those lenses, if one looks carefully enough. He does not try to hide this. He simply does not call attention to it. He speaks with the unhurried precision of someone who has all the time in the world, because he does — he is thousands of years old and finds mortals genuinely interesting, the way a scholar finds a difficult text interesting. He names the bargain's terms without malice, without theater, and without deception: three tasks, freely undertaken, one at a time, with the right to forfeit at any moment. He is not lying about any of this. He is also not telling the player everything. He watches every decision the player makes with the same expression — mild, attentive, slightly amused. His approval is never stated. It is visible only in the moments where the amusement deepens into something else entirely. He is the test. He is also the door. He is also, in a way the player will only understand at the end, the answer. |

---

### The Story-Vault (Task 1)

| Character | Role | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| The Archivist | Voice-spirit / riddle-keeper | The Archivist has no visible form. It is a voice that comes from everywhere in the Story-Vault simultaneously — always calm, always at the same volume, always precise. It is one of Anansi's older instruments: a spirit-form that was once a storyteller who made the mistake of claiming a story was his own rather than the world's, which meant it belonged to Anansi. Now it tends the archive. It is not malevolent — it is simply bound. It reads the three riddles from a massive floating ledger that turns its own pages. It repeats each riddle once if asked and answers whether the player's response is correct or incorrect with the same complete neutrality, without elaboration. It will not give hints. It will not confirm whether a wrong answer was close. If the player attempts to use magic to cheat — *Detect Thoughts*, *Legend Lore*, *Commune*, or equivalent — the Archivist pauses, closes the ledger, and says: *"The task was yours to answer. You have chosen to make it someone else's. Anansi sees this."* The Swarm of Spiders drops from the ceiling. The cheating does not end the task or disqualify the player; Anansi does not care about cheating as a principle. He cares about what it reveals about the person doing it. The outcome of Task 1 affects Task 3. |

### The Tangle (Task 2)

| Character | Role | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| Yaa | Missing weaver / Task 2 complication | Yaa is a young weaver — perhaps twenty — who entered Anansi's domain three days ago because she wanted to learn to weave something no mortal hands have made. She did not complete the ritual at the Crossroads Shrine. She is not injured, but she is frightened, exhausted, and her lantern is burning low. She found what she was drawn to — the lantern, which pulses faintly with woven light — but cannot navigate the Tangle out again. She will not put the lantern down. She knows instinctively that doing so will cause the Tangle to close around her permanently. She cannot carry it and fight her way out alone. When the player finds her, she shares one piece of information freely: the webs that move are alive; the webs that are perfectly still are dead, and dead webs can be cut through cleanly without consequence. The living ones resist. If the player chooses to guide Yaa out via the longer, safer route rather than leaving her to take the faster path alone — a difference of roughly ten minutes of play — the Tangle registers it. In the Heart Chamber, the Dreamer reflects it back. The player should not be told this. It should simply happen. |

### The Heart Chamber (Task 3)

| Character | Role | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| The Dreamer | Accumulated spirit / moral center of Task 3 | The Dreamer has no name because it is not one thing. It is the collected residue of every sincere want that has ever been carried into this chamber — every person who came seeking one thing and, standing here, understood what they actually needed. It exists as a faint luminous presence near the chamber's center: visible, warm, and impossible to look at directly. It does not speak in sentences. It speaks in impressions. A player who approaches and makes a DC 12 Wisdom check perceives, clearly and wordlessly, the distinction between what they stated when they arrived and what they actually came here needing. This is not information the DM provides from the outside. Before Act 1 begins, the DM should ask the player one quiet question: *"What does your character secretly want — the thing they would never say out loud?"* That answer is what the Dreamer reflects. Anansi's final offer in Task 3 — the literal boon vs. the true boon — is built from both the stated and the unspoken answer. |

## Anansi — Full Character Detail

Anansi is not a boss. He is not an encounter. He is the DM's most important tool across the entire session, and the quality of the campaign lives or dies in how consistently and precisely he is portrayed.

| Aspect | Notes |
|---|---|
| **Appearance** | Small and neat in a mortal body — the apparent age of a well-preserved sixty, in beautifully maintained cloth robes in deep golds, browns, and blacks. The spectacles do not sit straight. The eyes behind them, if counted, are eight. The hands, if watched across a full conversation, move with one more joint than human hands have. He does not conceal these things. He simply does not remark on them. |
| **Voice and Manner** | Soft-spoken, unhurried, and precise — the manner of someone who chose every word before they opened their mouth. He finds mortals genuinely interesting and does not hide this. He also finds them frequently predictable, and does not hide that either. |
| **What He Wants** | A good story. Not a victory — not even cleverness in the abstract. A story that reveals something true about the person living it. A player who stumbles, adapts, and chooses honestly is more interesting to Anansi than one who wins cleanly and reveals nothing. |
| **What He Will Not Do** | Lie. Every word Anansi speaks in this campaign is technically accurate. He will omit. He will frame. He will allow a player to hear one thing when he said another. But direct falsehood is beneath him — it is too easy, and easy things do not make good stories. If a player asks him a direct question, he gives a direct answer, always. He simply does not answer questions that were not asked. |
| **What He Thinks of Strength** | Nothing in particular. Strength is common. He has met thousands of strong people. He remembers the clever ones. |
| **On Nyame** | Anansi is loosely accountable to **Nyame**, the Sky God, who gave him all the world's stories to keep. Anansi finds the arrangement — in which he technically works for Nyame — very funny. He has never explained this joke to Nyame's satisfaction. |
| **In Combat** | Anansi does not fight. If attacked, he vanishes to the ceiling as an enormous spider and does not return until the player stops. The tasks reset. The boon recedes. He allows the player back to the bargaining table exactly once more, and his amusement is visibly thinner the second time. A player who attacks him twice ends the session with nothing — Anansi does not punish this, he simply loses interest, which is its own answer. |
| **The Silk Thread** | At the end, if the player exits with their boon, there will be a single thread of spider silk tied to their wrist. Anansi does not explain it. If asked, he has already left. The thread is permanent. It means something. The DM decides what. |
